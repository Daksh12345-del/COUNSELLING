What this data is, and isn't. These are minimum allocation scores — the lowest CSAS score at which a seat was actually allotted in that program, college and category. They are not CUET percentiles and not a University-issued predictor. Cutoffs commonly move down across the admission cycle as seats vacate, and a few evening colleges have since merged with parent colleges — always cross-check anything decision-critical against the official CSAS-DU portal before acting on it.

How the numbers work

Reading a CSAS cutoff sheet

01

It's a minimum, not a target

Each figure is the lowest score that still got a seat for that exact college, program and category — clearing it doesn't guarantee much margin, it's the floor.

02

Categories aren't ranked separately

UR, OBC-NCL, SC, ST, EWS and PwBD each have their own cutoff because seats are reserved by category — compare your score only against your own category's column.

03

Blank cells mean no seat filled

A missing cutoff for a category in a given program usually means no candidate from that category was allotted a seat there — not that the cutoff was zero.

04

Cutoffs shift over time

As admitted students migrate or withdraw, later phases of admission typically release seats at lower scores — a "close" result today can clear later in the cycle.
